I have an idea for a novel. It’s about a young girl who finds out that her mother is one of the most evil people to ever exist. Not because of anything specific she does but intrinsically. Her evilness is revealed through innocuous everyday activities and banal conversations. I think this would make a great book because it reveals the emotional conflicts involved when you absolutely and wholeheartedly love and hate the very same person, in equal measure, and the excruciating pain of learning that the person who is meant to love you unconditionally hates you vindictively because its their nature to hate. It would also delve into the insanely difficult moral dilemmas you’d face. All of this illustrated in the quintessential, and oftentimes fraught, mother-daughter relationship.

We know how to deal with other people’s attacks and criticism, but it’s different when someone who is meant to love you hates you—It catches you completely off-guard so even the smallest hint of malice does a lot of damage. It is like your own shadow stabbing you in the back one day. But there’s one upside to it too: you become largely immune to outside criticism. My own experience of something similar made it so I find it cute when people try to give me a hard time now. I really do. I’ve seen people suddenly get a shiver down their spine when they realized that I’m completely unaffected by their malice. Their attacks just don’t register as pain because I’ve heard way worse—not necessarily in terms of content, but in terms of who delivered it. It took a lot to get there emotionally, but it was perhaps due to, or as a result of, my sense of humor. Every time you overcome pain, it expands your sense of humor. 1

I’m not saying that I’m this cyborg that doesn’t feel emotional pain. I do. Pain is universal. Let me give you an example of just how fragile I can be. Nothing in life prepared me for my first unsubscribe. It stung! I logged into my email one morning and saw ‘substack’ and ‘subscriber’. For about a second and a half, my heart skipped a beat. I though it was a new subscriber. Then I saw those two words ‘email disabled’—gut punch. My heart sunk and I immediately tried to positively reassure myself. “Maybe they don’t want emails because they prefer to read via Substack.” “It’s not personal, they are probably getting a lot of emails and just want to reduce their overall subscriptions.” But I decided, correctly or not, that they were probably a pissed off reader. I couldn’t help but think I must have done or said the wrong thing and pissed them off.

I’ve grown a lot since that day and nowadays, I’d much rather someone unsubscribe once they realize Quant (h)Edge isn’t for them, and more so if I hit a nerve. The late great comedian Patrice O’Neal used to say that everybody laughing at a joke is not a sign of good comedy; out of 100 people, 50 should be laughing and the other 50 should be horrified. I think good writing or art is like that. It comforts the disturbed, and disturbs the comfortable. What you as a reader don’t want is someone who says and does the right things to keep the readership happy. Even if such a thing is possible, it would simply be dishonest. I’ve come to learn that all I can do here is be honest in my pursuit of edge, and in writing about what I see in the markets, or what I think about different stuff. The same place where the good ideas come from also churns out crap like this. Anyway, point is, I bruise just as easily as anyone else, but not on direct attacks or criticism. If you ask me, I think having had to overcome the pain of being attacked by so-called loved ones expanded my sense of humor so I can take a personal hit.

A sense of humor is something real inside of you. It’s not only about what you find to be funny, but is part of your general outlook on life and how you process life’s events. The beautiful thing about a sense of humor is that it operates beyond your usual defenses so it is authentic to our true selves and our true emotional experience of things. A sense of humor is primal, instinctive and completely honest. You can’t help what you find funny. I don’t know if you’ve seen that Simpsons episode where Homer laughs at a college professor. Here it is just incase.

I’m convinced that the reason we find something funny is because we feel complete power over or safety from it. Take Homer in the above clip for example. You could say he finds the idea of someone who is supposed to be the paragon of order making a silly and childish mistake. But another explanation could be that he can see himself as the professor and imagine the absolute pain and humiliation of something like that happening to him, but then he realizes that actually it’s not happening to the real him but to someone else. A good sense of humor comes from empathy. Psychopaths for example don’t have a good sense of humor. Their sense of humor is different. If you want to make a psychopath laugh, you are better off putting on the scariest and most violent horror movie you can find. Jeffrey Dahmer’s favorite movie was “The Exorcist III”. But in terms of being able to find humor in themselves and their life, they don’t have much of that.

It is said that laughter is the best medicine. Even in finance that is true. Any time you put on a trade, or buy a stock, you invest two capitals: your financial capital, and your emotional capital. Sometimes, the financial capital is small, but the emotional capital is enormous. Money is funny because we value it based on our feelings. A dollar is a dollar anywhere, but then again not really. No matter how much of it you have, you always think that more money would solve your current problems. A group of Harvard researchers asked 2,000 millionaires with fortunes ranging $1-$50 million how much money it would take for them to be comfortable. Their response? Twice what I have. But of course it isn’t true. Every new tax bracket you cross comes with new problems and those problems make you anxious, which will make you think more that money could help.

Jared Dillian put me onto the idea that money problems are the best problems to have. He is absolutely right. Money problems are good because they are for the most part solvable, kinda like math problems. In math, you define your problem and if your algebra is good, you will at some point put two dashes and write down the correct solution. Problem solved. Money problems are the similar. Whatever it is, you can always think of a few things you could do or stop doing to solve it. You can even write them down, put an equal (or an approx.) sign and write down a range of expected outcomes. Better still, the success of your solution often depends on your ability to do what you need to do, and you can control you. Nonfinancial problems are way more difficult to solve. Relationship problems are hard because you can’t solve them alone—you need the other person to do their part too. Business problems depend on a lot of factors, many of which you have little to no control over. Health problems depend on so many things from your attitude to the quality of healthcare you have access to, and most importantly, whether there’s even a cure or not.

A few people I’ve explained this to have couldn’t help but chuckle at the iron-clad argument. I think the humor is in the realization that what makes financial problems hard is how absurdly emotional we get about them. Once you realize this it cuts the problem down to size, and that right there is the power of a sense of humor. It changes your perspective on things and helps you transform pain into power.

In Harry Potter there are these things called boggarts that shape-shift into your worst fear. It’s usually something obvious like a spider or your favorite Professor telling you that you have failed an exam. But in real life, a boggart would be way scarier because few of us really know our biggest fears. Here is where I marveled at J.K Rowling’s brilliance. Since Harry Potter was a book about magic, she could have invented any kind of magical spell, charm or potion to counter a boggart. But she chose laughter as the main component—something basic to all humans and not exclusive to witches and wizards. All someone needs to do to thwart a boggart is think of something that makes them laugh really hard, invoke the Riddikulus spell to conjure it up and once the humor kicks in and they truly laugh, the boggart loses its power. She must have realized at some point in her life that when you are scared, like really scared, nothing is more powerful than your ability to laugh, truly laugh, at whatever is causing you so much fear and pain. Novels are great because you learn so much without having to consciously think about stuff, and I’m sure her wisdom passed on to many kids who had to face boggarts in real life.

In finance, the boggart is losing it all, or at least a significant chunk of it. Can you laugh your way out of that? Yes, but it depends on how strong your sense of humor is. Some people have lost a lot of money in crypto recently. The dollar amounts vary but the emotional toll is the same: maximum pain. I truly feel for them because I see myself in them. I love crypto traders because they embody the raison d'être of investing. No one is here to simply pay their bills and drive a cheap-ass car. We are all here for the chance to someday make life-changing money for ourselves and those we love. Crypto guys are simply willing to take on more risk to get there than most people can handle. So tradFi guys don’t get to be self-righteous right now that crypto is under water. None of that talk that ‘crypto is gambling’. Remember when Druckenmiller lost $3 billion in the 2000 tech bubble? What was that if not gambling?

Martin Shkreli, who often posts some fire ideas, was a victim of the recent Altcoin bloodbath. Now there’s a guy with a great sense of humor. He posted this tweet a couple of weeks ago. He probably has an above-average pain tolerance and risk tolerance, and you can see why he is more likely to recover from such a thing than the guy who won’t even look at his portfolio when its down.

I saw a reddit post of a guy who lost $90k in the onslaught and was thinking of ending it all. Some of the comments were people who had lost more, and they tried explaining that that 90k wasn’t that bad. But they missed the point. It’s not the 90k, it’s the emotional distress. It’s feeling that you failed those you love and are supposed to take care of. It’s feeling like the dumbest person alive and wondering if your dream of financial freedom is well and truly dead.

If that sounds like you, you’re looking at it all wrong. You need to find the humor in the situation. Seriously. I don’t mean a pyrotechnic exercise of trying to laugh at something you naturally don’t find funny. I mean taking a step back, waaay back, out of yourself, and seeing the folly of this very human situation you find yourself in. I mean finding some good cheer despite what has happened. A loss is not a loss, it’s a lesson. Every trade gives you either a profit or a lesson, that’s it. Every trade.

If you ever experience a huge loss, know that you’ll be fine. Even if you lose it all and actually have to work at a bullshit job to make ends meet, you’ll be fine. Time heals everything and a sense of humor helps the healing. Stand back and laugh at your portfolio, laugh at yourself, laugh at the situation you are in. Take the sting out of it so you can better face the future and make better decisions. Do it because you will make money again in future. Even if it will not be the same in dollar terms, it will sure as hell feel that way. Don’t run from the pain, run towards it and turn it into something beautiful. A sense of humor gives you back your emotional capital, and maybe even increases it.
