I love learning something new that could improve my life. It has made me a sucker to advice. I don’t read self-help books, but if I come across a new worldview or advice about something pertinent to me, I immediately try it out. So yes, I did read ‘The Secret’, and yes, I did made a ‘vision board’. But I stopped believing it worked along with everyone else, and nowadays I am skeptical of anything to do with ‘energies’ or ‘vibrations’, even though I am very tolerant of people who do.

At the same time, I generally dislike advice, especially if it comes in the form of short, clever remarks. I prefer advice in book form, or at the very least an essay. If it sounds exciting or revolutionary enough, I will try it out. I also dislike giving advice. I feel like you just come across as patronizing, which generates ill will, and people become defensive so your advice falls on deaf ears; this is often how I react to trading advice. Also, most advice sounds like things you already know, even if you’ve never put them into words. Trading advice especially feels like it’s pointing a finger at you saying “You know this, yet you don’t do it. This is why you are not as successful as you could be.” But trading is funny because you need the advice, even if you know. It goes to your subconscious and influences you to do the right things when the situation arises.

I don’t usually make New Years resolutions because I know there’s little chance I will follow through—I have a hard time following lists, schedules, et cetera. But this year, I consciously identified several things I could change this year to make me happier. After reviewing my trading results from last year, not that I trade a serious account, I noted two main reason behind my losing trades: a bad attitude and not thinking independently. Generally, I dislike advice. If you want to give me advice, you have to first write a book. I will read it, try out your stuff, and if it doesn’t work out, toss it in the bin (glad to say that the only book I’ve ever thrown away is ‘The Secret’). Nowadays I struggle to find books to read because they lack true originality. What substitutes for originality nowadays is to take a tried-and-tested idea and change a few parameters. It’s not just in books where originality has become rare. Netflix sometimes make basically the same movie over and over again; academic studies have devolved to rotating among a few ideas, perhaps changing the dataset; cars all look basically the same nowadays—you catch my drift.

As much as I dislike taking advice, I dislike giving it. It often comes off as if you think you are better than others. People are like ‘Who are you to be giving me advice? What qualifies you?’ People put up a wall of defense, so even if your advice is the antidote to their problem, it falls on deaf ears. Ill will and deaf ears, that’s what you get. It’s a bad proposition so I don’t do it—well, unless I think you about to make a huge mistake.

All this to say, please forgive my intrusion for giving you any advice; it’s not that I like doing it, or that I think I’m any better (if anything I think more highly of you). I only do it to provoke your thinking so you can discover what you think and find your own way.

I am convinced that, assuming you are a decent enough trader already, a good attitude and independent analysis are the difference between a profitable and unprofitable year.

A bad attitude compounds all problems, not just in trading, but in life. Everyone knows that if you want to improve your life, first improve your attitude. Everyone knows how a good attitude can make everything much better. But few of us actually catch ourselves being sour and go out of our way to change our attitude. If anything, we become defensive to any attempts to ‘cheer us up’. The more anyone tries, the more annoyed by them we get. But in trading, the market pulls out its whip and beats you if you remain sour, only you never notice that this is what’s happening. Instead, you end up attributing your poor performance to technical details: “I entered too early”, “I followed bad trading advice”, “I overleveraged”… Yet, an external observer, if they know you well enough, can often see what’s happening a lot more clearly. Maybe they pull you to the side and ask you if everything is okay at home, or with the wife, they try to find out what has you in such a bad mood lately. Often, it’s a few bad trades that started it. Isaac Newton famously said: “I can calculate the motion of heavenly bodies but not the madness of people”, after losing a vast portion of his wealth in the South Sea Company bubble crash. Attitude problem.

The funny thing about trading with a bad attitude is that you can still do everything right and get poor results. As a result of your bad mood, somehow, something invariably goes wrong, which pushes you further in a bad state. A bad attitude also makes you dogmatic—you start to dismiss things that can help you! It is almost like you are stuck in a vortex of negativity, as if everything conspires to ruin you.

Everyone has a baseline attitude that is either positive or negative. As a trader, you want to err on the positive side at all cost.

Attitudes are contagious, you can catch them from someone near you, or transmit them to others. I have learnt the hard way to avoid people who are negative, even if they are not treating me poorly. There are a few exceptions, people I go through great lengths to cheer up, but more often than not, I will find any excuse to avoid you if you are in a bad mood.

I’ve noticed that social media tends to put me in a bad mood so I use it with caution. Nowadays, social media is an input for investing. When I started trading in 2018, social media didn’t matter. You looked at the calendar, followed economic developments, read some research, and did your technical analysis. Nowadays, I spend pay attention to the narratives circulating on social media. There are tradeoffs. On the one hand, you get timely information, on the other hand, you become vulnerable to other people’s biases and attitudes.

The age of social media has also promoted a culture where there are fewer independent thinkers. Instead, people look for the consensus. That is detrimental to your bottom line. Risk-taking induces anxiety, and people try to feel safe by getting into trades that everyone else is involved in. People especially look for ‘gurus’, experts in specific assets that they can then follow. What such people don’t realize is that even real experts don’t have a secret source of information that they are not privy to. Trading is not a team sport, you eat what you and you alone kill.
