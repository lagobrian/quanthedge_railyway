I’ve been calculating the VIX for a bunch of assets and I’m glad to say that I’ve succeeded! It’s been quite stressful because A: I got the data from a pretty random site (optionsdx.com) and I wasn’t confident it would any good(I could only get data from 2016 to end of 2023 for everything else apart from SPX (2010-2023).), and B: the coding was so tricky. I started off in python but had to switch to R after a while because python was taking forever and would stop if there was a whitespace somewhere in the data. The R code is actually quite fast and if you have a multicore machine, you can run the whole thing in less than an hour. There is some more analyses to do, and I will add that code here after I’m done. Here’s a preview of the data.

The first sanity check is the VIX (white) vs my calculations (red). The chart above shows that they basically overlap, even though my one has a few overshoots. It at least shows that I did the correct thing. Those overshoots are due to difficulties picking the next term options for the calculation prior to Oct 6th, 2018, when CBOE changed the VIX methodology to select weekly options expiring after 23 days and before 37 days for the ‘near term’ and ‘next term’ chains. That’s why after that date the two series basically overlap from that date onwards.  I added SPY’s VIX (blue) to contrast it to the main index’s. My current thinking is that there is no real reason SPY’s VIX should overlap with SPX’s VIX because one tracks the other and can have some minor divergences, plus the option chains are different, but interestingly, they do overlap (and SPY’s VIX has no overshoots).

The chart shows QQQ’s VIX (red) vs VXD (blue) and VIX (white). The series follow similar trajectories, but QQQ’s VIX hovers above the rest. I will say more about this in the upcoming post.

Next, I did equity VIXs. CBOE publishes some equity VIXs like VXAPL (AAPL) and VXAZN (AMZN). I did a sanity check by recalculating the AAPL VIX and comparing it to VXAPL. The chart is shown below.

Again, some overshoots before Oct 6th, 2018, and then they overlap afterwards. I added the VIX here to contrast the idiosyncratic equity VIX to the market VIX. Interesting to see that the equity VIX is higher than the market VIX so maybe using the market VIX to guage a stock’s volatility could be deceptive, and this is one of the things I wanted to know when I undertook this exercise.

Being confident that my code was correct, and my data was good enough, I created a Tesla VIX and an Nvidia VIX to further investigate the dynamics of idiosyncratic VIXs. The Tesla VIX, shown above, is quite high compared to any other VIX, including Nvidia’s. It touched 100 twelve times! Nvidia’s VIX is shown below, and there is one huge spike that ruins the y-axis. I don’t know if that is an anomaly yet because as I’ve shown, my code and data are correct, but I will look into it and report more about it in the upcoming post.
