Welcome to the first edition of the Quant (h)Edge weekly market report. This report covers the Week of 30th September-4th October.

The Big Story last week was the resurgence of US treasury yields which finished with big moves on Friday following a blowout jobs report. NFPs increased by 254k versus 147k estimate and 159k prev. During the week, 2yrs moved from 3.57% to 3.81%, 5yrs moved from 4.49% to 4.505%, 10yrs moved from 3.802% to 3.981% and 30yrs moved from 4.134% to 4.267%.

Performance

Here’s what other assets did last week.

Indices

Last week, indices were either down or up slightly apart from the Euro Stoxx 50 and IPC Mexico which gained 7.6% and 1.9% respectively. The IPC Mexico index has been down 5.3% in the past three months, and -0.2% over the past month, so last week's performance shows a change in direction for the index. Euro Stoxx 50 has gained 29.7% over the past three months and 28.5% over the past month mostly due to the ECB cutting rates, the market expecting more cuts, and the big Chinese stimulus expected to help European exports. Other indices were down or unchanged due to the geopolitical tensions.

Commodities

Oil and Heating Oil were the best-performing commodities last week gaining 9.2% and 6.7% respectively. The two commodities are highly correlated as usual— both have been on the ascent over the past month gaining 5.8% and 5.9% respectively. Oil went up last week due to tensions between Israel and Iran, who fired at each other. The commodity had bottomed a month ago partly due to the dissipation of recession fears when they signaled a 50bps cut.

NatGas was down last week after gaining 28.5% over the past month after weekly data by Baker Hughes showed that natural gas rigs in the US had increased by three (WSJ).

Gold has made notable gains this year and is up 13.5% in the past three months. However, in the past month it has gained 7.4% and last week it was up by 1.4%.

Crypto

Last week was the first week of 'Uptober', a moniker given to October by crypto investors because Bitcoin tends to rise in October. However, most coins were down last week. Bitcoin shed 5.1% and Ethereum shed 3.1%. Litecoin was the worst-performing cryptocurrency last week shedding 12.6%.

Currencies

In currencies, last week's best performer was USDJPY which gained 4.1% followed by CADJPY which gained 3.6%. The worst performers were NZDUSD (down 3%), GBPUSD (down 2.0%), and EURUSD (down 1.8%). The main factor in these moves was a widening of the rate differentials due to the rising US yields. The EU and BOE are now expected to cut more than the Fed. Also, the new Japanese Prime Minister said that he didn't think the economy was ready for further rate hikes, which sent the Yen hurtling. CAD moved due to oil; this correlation had broken down after the US became one of the major oil exporters, but it was back last week after oil surged due to the Israel-Iran tensions.

Commodities

Volatility across the commodity space was lower last week except for Soybean oil. There were notable decreases in volatility for Cocoa, NatGas, Silver, Orange Juice, and Soybean futures.

Currencies

In FX, volatility expanded for the JPY crosses, especially USDJPY. There were also small upticks in volatility for EURAUD, EURCAD, GBPCAD, and EURGBP.

Crypto

Last week, volatility picked up for most cryptocurrencies compared to the one month.

Trading Ranges

Trading ranges and volatility are related but they are not the same thing. That volatility increased or decreased last week doesn’t say much about the average daily trading range hence I included this section.

Indices

Most indices move about 0.8-1.1% daily if you look back using one-week, one-month, or three-month data. But last week, the S&P 500, FTSE 100, Euro Stoxx 50, Nikkei, Hang Seng, and Euronext 100 saw upticks in their average daily trading ranges. Euro Stoxx 50 moved by 4.2% on average each day, which is higher than the 2.2% average trading range over the past month. Nikkei's average trading range last week was 1.8% which is higher than the 1.5% estimate over the past month. Analogously, the S&P 500's average daily range last week was 1.3% versus 1.1% over the past month.

Commodities

Cocoa's trading range increased last week to 6.9% daily on average versus 4.2% on the month. Coffee, Palladium, Oat, and Soybean futures also saw a notable widening of their average daily trading ranges.

Currencies

In the FX space, JPY crosses saw their average daily ranges increase. AUDNZD had the largest increase in it’s average daily range last week from 0.9% over the past month to 1.8% last week.

Crypto

In cryptocurrencies, there were notable increases in the average daily range for Shiba Inu, Doge, and XRP.

Correlations

Indices vs Currencies

Looking at the correlation between indices (y-axis) and currencies (x-axis), we see that the JPY crosses have been strongly positively correlated to the IPC Mexico index. I have no clue why that is and will have to look into that one.

The ASX 200 seems to significantly affect AUD pairs with positive correlations ranging from 27% to 40% across the currencies with AUD as the numerator or denominator.

Nikkei’s strongest correlation is its inverse correlation to the EURUSD (rho=-40%), followed by inverse correlations to GBPUSD(rho=-37%), AUDUSD (rho=-33%) and AUDCAD (rho=-30%).

Commodities and Currencies

Oil, Gold, Silver, Copper, Heating Oil, NatGas, Corn, Cotton, and Sugar have strong correlations to JPY crosses looking back on the month. Cocoa and Orange Juice are inversely correlated to AUD pairs.

Commodities and Indices

Commodities are strongly correlated to the Euro Stoxx 50 index. It seems like the market is already pricing in a rebound in Europe. Palladium is positively correlated with all indices in what is likely a Chinese stimulus play. The metal has a 52% correlation with the Hang Seng index and a 54% correlation with the Euronext 100 index. Orange Juice is negatively correlated to all twelve indices.

Silver, Gold, and Copper have weakly positive correlations to the FTSE 100, S&P 500, and the Nasdaq indices. Coffee has stronger correlations than the metals to all three indices.
