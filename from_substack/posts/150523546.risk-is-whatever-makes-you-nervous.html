The SNP has been relentless. In the 16 years since ‘08, it has only had four down years. It’s nearly a year since Fed Waller signaled to markets that the Fed would cut rates in about three or four months and the SNP took off, gaining more than 30% since then.

In 2007, Warren Buffet made an open challenge of $1M in his shareholder letter that an SNP fund would outperform any basket of five hedge funds selected by the counterparty. His thesis was that since hedge fund fees cost more than an index fund, hedge funds have to start from behind and do a lot just to catch up to the index fund. His bet was taken up by Ted Seides, then a founding partner and hedge fund manager of Protégé Partners. Buffett won the bet, and with eight months remaining until the end of the 10 years, Ted wrote about why he lost. He agreed with Buffett that fees did indeed play a part, but more importantly, the S&P 500 had changed post-crisis—it simply went up.

Hitherto it’s been fun, but it’s starting to make people nervous. Morning Brew recently posted a video on YouTube arguing that the SNP keeps going up because pople just buy it regardless of the cost—they get their paycheck, pay their bills, and then put a large chunk of the excess in the SNP at whatever the prevailing price is. In the video, they show that passive investing has become more prevalent than active investing, and it is seen as a retirement saving slash get-rich-slow mechanism. The answer to the above question is “No, it won’t keep going up forever”. The SNP keeps going up for the same reason Gold and Bitcoin (despite crazy drawdowns) keep going up—hodlers.

The thesis for Buffet’s bet is something he first laid out in his 1996 shareholder letter when he first said that most investors will find that index investing is the best way to gain exposure to stocks due to the low fees. In 2002, someone asked him about this and he reiterated the same view. Charlie Munger on the other hand, in response to this point said that at some point, the index will become so expensive that some poor SOB will top-tick it for a long time somewhat similar to what happened to the Nikkei after 1990. This is why, according to Guiseppe Paleologo (Gappy), people invest in hedge funds. Gappy makes the point that people invest in hedge funds despite them underperforming the SNP because hedge fund returns are meant to offer higher quality returns. It is not the magnitude of the returns that matters but that they are uncorrelated with the SNP and institutional investors and have lower volatility. And when shit hits the fan, those few dollars the hedge funds make when everyone else is losing money will mean the world. That’s why you go to quant school and learn all that tosh about variance-covariance matrices.

So perhaps Buffett won his bet not because his thesis was right, but because the real thesis was that passive investing would become popular for the next 30 or so years. Buffett’s edge is that he understands consumer behavior, and index investing is a form of consumption.

What you have with Gold, the SNP and Bitcoin are hodlers. What differentiates them is that each is motivated by different ideals and outlooks on life. For those who see everything one day going to hell, and want to preserve their wealth in that scenario, there’s gold; for those who work stable jobs and save and want to be rich someday in the future, there’s the SNP; and for those who want to strike it rich and live a lavish lifestyle in a new world where the system finally comes to its knees, there’s Bitcoin (or some other crypto). What the hodlers have in common is that they are willing to stomach volatility.

Warren Buffett is a hodler. One of his most common rants is about how volatility is not risk. He likes to illustrate his point by arguing that if you got a live price for an acre of land, and it was volatile, you wouldn’t change your view about the value of the land with the market because you understood that it would produce the same output regardless of the price. What you would be looking for as prices bobbed about was how cheap you could buy it so you could buy more. This is how hodlers view their chosen asset but the question is whether they are right about the real value of their holdings. That is, whether they truly understand the risks. Take the equity hodlers for example. I recently wrote about what causes market crashes. Even if indices don’t become too expensive, stocks are still vulnerable to crashes (read the article to find out why).

This happened to me recently. I’ve mentioned before that my forays into trading involve putting tiny amounts in an FX account on and off. It’s better than using a paper account to learn as I save up for a real account. Going into last month’s ECB meeting, I was long the EURUSD and long oil for different reasons that I had written in my journal. The oil trade was going well (somehow my non-FX trades are usually unproblematic) so my focus was on the ECB meeting. I was basically handicapping ECB’s Lagarde because she is poor at communicating and I was quite confident she would come off more hawkish than the market expected (25bps not 50 and somewhat hawkish guidance). Despite this view, I whiffed in the last moments before the rate decision was published and got flat. A few days later, I flipped my bias to a short bias due to economic weakness in Germany and the view that the ECB would be forced to be more dovish than they were in the meeting while the Fed would have to be more hawkish after delivering 50bps. I thought the Eurodollar was at the top so I waited for some form of confirmation that it was starting to go lower before I went short. My stop was miles away at 1.12ish and I was looking for 1.1095. It was a brilliant trade in my view. Cue the above meme (caused by the China stimulus).

What was painful about that trade is that I got stopped out at the top tick and then the Eurodollar did exactly what I wanted it to. Anyway, while this was going on, I also got jiggled out of the oil trade.

I usually don’t have a problem not fiddling with stop losses once I set them, but I am often eager to get out of trades quickly. If I was part of the Ackman-Cathie memes, I would be the guy who got out at 2x thinking I’ve done a good job, only for the thing to go to the moon. So while this article may come across as a diatribe against hodlers, it is the opposite; I admire their emotional strength, imagination about expected returns and commitment to views. This post was actually going to be about the emotional strength needed to endure volatility but it took a while to get here.

So yeah, I do admire the hodlers. They may not have to worry about stop-losses unless they are trading on margin like me, but what they do have to stomach is volatility. Volatility is how you trade crypto as far as I can tell—you buy it and then you hodl for as long as it takes to rally significantly, and you only get out when it is clear that the rally is over. (Read more about why that is so here).

What the hodlers have that I don’t is emotional fitness. Emotional fitness is a weird thing. With physical fitness, you have to train a muscle to make it stronger and it can only bear as much burden as you have trained it for. But emotional fitness depends on so many things from genetics to past traumas, upbringing, character, temperament, stress levels, and whether you slept enough and had breakfast. I’ll tell you one thing though, you need to develop emotional strength to be a profitable trader. Volatility makes you do stupid stuff because you are not strong enough emotionally. It’s called risk tolerance and most people don’t have as much of it as they think they do. The next time the SNP drops significantly, some people will have to find this out the hard way.

Emotional strength is not something you turn on and off again but a different way of looking at the world. It’s taking the cold shower, it’s doing the thing you keep postponing, it’s not reacting when someone is pushing your buttons.

If volatility isn’t risk, then what is risk? Risk is whatever makes you nervous. The fewer the things that can make you nervous, the more risk you can tolerate.
